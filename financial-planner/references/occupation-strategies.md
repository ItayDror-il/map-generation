# Occupation-Based Financial Strategies

## Income Stability Tiers

### Tier 1: High Stability (Score: 1.0)
**Occupations:** Government employees, tenured professors, executives with contracts, utility workers, healthcare (nurses, doctors)

**Characteristics:**
- Predictable paycheck
- Strong job security
- Often includes pension/benefits
- Low unemployment risk

**Financial strategy:**
- Emergency fund: 3 months expenses
- Can take more investment risk
- Aggressive debt payoff acceptable
- Can commit to fixed expenses confidently

| Allocation | Recommended |
|------------|-------------|
| Emergency fund | 3 months |
| Investment risk | Higher (80-90% stocks when young) |
| Debt strategy | Aggressive payoff |
| Fixed commitments | Can commit confidently |

---

### Tier 2: Professional Stability (Score: 0.8)
**Occupations:** Engineers, accountants, lawyers, IT professionals, business analysts, scientists

**Characteristics:**
- Generally stable but subject to market conditions
- Layoffs possible during downturns
- Transferable skills
- Good re-employment prospects

**Financial strategy:**
- Emergency fund: 4 months expenses
- Moderate investment risk
- Balance debt payoff with savings
- Maintain marketable skills

| Allocation | Recommended |
|------------|-------------|
| Emergency fund | 4 months |
| Investment risk | Moderate (70-80% stocks when young) |
| Debt strategy | Balanced approach |
| Skill investment | Budget for certifications/training |

---

### Tier 3: Trade/Service Stability (Score: 0.6)
**Occupations:** Electricians, plumbers, mechanics, retail managers, restaurant managers, skilled trades

**Characteristics:**
- Employment tied to economic cycles
- Local market dependent
- Physical demands may limit career length
- Income can vary with overtime

**Financial strategy:**
- Emergency fund: 4-5 months expenses
- Moderate-conservative investment risk
- Prioritize income protection
- Consider disability insurance

| Allocation | Recommended |
|------------|-------------|
| Emergency fund | 4-5 months |
| Investment risk | Moderate (60-70% stocks) |
| Debt strategy | Steady, consistent |
| Insurance | Disability coverage important |

---

### Tier 4: Variable Income (Score: 0.4)
**Occupations:** Sales (commission), real estate agents, freelancers, gig workers, artists, consultants

**Characteristics:**
- Income fluctuates monthly/quarterly
- Feast-or-famine cycles
- No employer benefits typically
- High earning potential but unpredictable

**Financial strategy:**
- Emergency fund: 6 months expenses minimum
- Conservative base, opportunistic additions
- Budget based on lowest expected income
- Smooth income through reserves

| Allocation | Recommended |
|------------|-------------|
| Emergency fund | 6+ months |
| Investment risk | Conservative base (50-60% stocks) |
| Debt strategy | Conservative, prioritize cash flow |
| Income smoothing | Essential - "pay yourself" consistent amount |

**Income smoothing strategy:**
1. All income goes to business/income account
2. Calculate sustainable monthly "salary"
3. Transfer fixed amount to personal account monthly
4. Build surplus in good months
5. Draw from surplus in lean months

---

### Tier 5: Seasonal/Cyclical (Score: 0.2)
**Occupations:** Construction workers, tourism/hospitality, agriculture, tax preparers, retail (seasonal)

**Characteristics:**
- Predictable busy/slow seasons
- May have unemployment periods
- Income concentrated in peak months
- Physical or weather dependent

**Financial strategy:**
- Emergency fund: 6-9 months expenses
- Very conservative approach
- Must budget annually, not monthly
- Save heavily during peak season

| Allocation | Recommended |
|------------|-------------|
| Emergency fund | 6-9 months |
| Investment risk | Conservative (40-50% stocks) |
| Debt strategy | Very conservative |
| Seasonal reserve | Separate from emergency fund |

**Seasonal budgeting:**
1. Calculate annual income realistically
2. Divide by 12 for monthly budget
3. During peak: save 40-50% of income
4. During off-season: draw from seasonal reserve
5. Keep seasonal reserve separate from emergency fund

## Occupation-Specific Considerations

### Healthcare Workers
- Often have access to 403(b) plans
- May have pension options
- Disability insurance critical
- Student loan forgiveness programs (PSLF)

### Tech Workers
- Stock compensation common (RSUs, options)
- Diversify away from company stock
- High income but volatile industry
- Consider concentration risk

### Teachers/Educators
- Pension systems vary by state
- 403(b) plans available
- Summer income planning needed
- PSLF eligible (public schools)

### Small Business Owners
- See [business-allocation.md](business-allocation.md)
- SEP-IRA or Solo 401k essential
- Separate business/personal finances
- Liability protection important

### Military/Veterans
- TSP (Thrift Savings Plan) - excellent low-cost option
- Pension after 20 years
- VA benefits planning
- Transition planning critical

### Gig Economy (Uber, DoorDash, etc.)
- Track all expenses meticulously
- Quarterly estimated taxes required
- Vehicle depreciation planning
- No benefits - budget for health insurance
